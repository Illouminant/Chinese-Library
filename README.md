<h2 style="color: seashell;" align="center"> Important Note on Tones</h2>
<div align="center">
	<p style="color: seashell;" align="center">
	    Tone 1: <b>_</b>
		    <br>
		Tone 2: <b>/</b>
		    <br>
		Tone 3: <b>v</b>
		    <br>
		Tone 4: <b>\</b>
		    <br><br>
		<b>Tone 1 ( - )</b> = <i>"The Singing Tone".</i> Keep at your level.
		    <br>
        <b>Tone 2 ( / )</b> = <i>"The Unsure Tone".</i> Like you're asking a question. "Yes?"
		    <br>
        <b>Tone 3 ( v )</b> = <i>"Full Third Tone"</i> or <i>"The Zombie Tone".</i> Full Third Tone is the full accentuation that you only say if the word is by itself. Otherwise you use The Zombie Tone, where your voice goes down and fizzles out at the bottom like a zombie. "Braains..."
		    <br>
        <b>Tone 4 ( \ )</b> = <i>"The Assertive Tone".</i> Like you're scolding something. "No."
		    <br>
        <b>Tone 5</b> = <i>"The Contrarian Tone".</i> No symbols above the letters. Very short, snappy and neutral.
	</p>
</div>

<button class="collapse" align="center">
	Additional note 一
</button>
    <p class="content" align="center">
		Tones are incredibly important in Chinese, as they symbolise the pitch of your voice whenever you say a word.
		    <br>
		Without tones, it can sound like you're saying something entirely different and confuse Chinese people.
		    <br><br>
		If you cannot type the tone, you should include a number after the word to signify what tone you meant.
		    <br>
		Even in typing, not using tones is incredibly confusing.
	</p>

<button class="collapse" align="center">
	Additional note 一
</button>
<p class="content" align="center">
	When asking 哪里 (Nǎlǐ), if the context is clear you can skip mentioning it.
		<br>
	It is also of note that 儿 (Er) tends to be used in place of 里 (Lǐ) in Northern China.
		<br>
	里 (Lǐ) is also considered formal and the variant used in writing.
		<br>
	Example: Lou在吗？(Lou zài ma?) = Lou is around?
</p>

<button class="collapse" align="center">
	Additional note 二
</button>
<p class="content" align="center">
	Words such as 半 (Bàn) and 月 (Yuè) are appended at the end of words to give them meaning.<br>
	Example: 五点半 (Wǔ diǎn bàn) means "half-past five", and 六月 (Liù yuè) means June. And yes, all months in Chinese are just their advent number + 月!
</p>

<button class="collapse" align="center">
	Additional note 三
</button>
<p class="content" align="center">
	There is a different between when you should use 要 (Yào) and 想 (Xiǎng). 要 (Yào) means to want, while 想 (Xiǎng) means thinking of wanting. So 要 (Yào) is a definitive want, while 想 (Xiǎng) could be subject to change.
</p>

<button class="collapse" align="center">
	Additional note 一
</button>
<p class="content" align="center">
	Numbers in Chinese are very literal, they just append to each other.
		<br>
	零 (Líng) works by showing the literal amount of values there would in a number.
		<br>
	Time works the exact same way, with 点 (Diǎn) placed where the : would be.
		<br>
	Finally, phone numbers are listed individually.
		<br>
	Example 一: 四十 (Sìshí) = 40. 十四 (Shísì) = 14.
		<br>
	Example 二: 一百 (Yībǎi) = 100. 四百四十 (Sìbǎi sìshí) = 440. 一千两百三十四 (Yīqiān liǎngbǎi sānshísì) = 1234.
		<br>
	Example 三: 一百零四 (Yībǎi líng sì) = 104. 一千零四十 (Yīqiān líng sìshí) = 1040.
		<br>
	Example 四: 十二点十 (Shí'èr diǎn shí) = 12:10, the time I wrote this!
</p>
<button class="collapse" align="center">
	Additional note 二
</button>
<p class="content" align="center">
    幺 is one when listing numbers and/or in noisy environments.<br>两 is two when it's a couple and/or two when it's referring to multiple of itself.
	    <br>
	Example 一: 我的电话号码是幺二三...... (Wǒ de diànhuà hàomǎ shì yāo èrsān......) = My phone number is one, two, three, etc...
		<br>
	Example 二: 两只猫 (Liǎng zhī māo) = Two cats.
		<br>
	Example 三: 两百 (Liǎng bǎi) = 200.
</p>

	星期 (Xīngqí) is only one, colloquial way of naming the weekdays. It is also just as valid to say 周 (Zhōu) in its place.
